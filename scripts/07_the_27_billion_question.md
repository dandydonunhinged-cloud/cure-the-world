## SCRIPT

In the last six videos, I showed you how a potential Alzheimer's cure was killed for profit. Every claim sourced. Every receipt on screen.

Now I'm going to show you something bigger. The system that made it possible.

In fiscal year 2024, the United States spent twenty-seven billion dollars on foreign military aid. Twenty-seven billion. For weapons, defense systems, and military support sent to other countries.

That same year, enhanced ACA subsidies — the thing keeping twenty-two million Americans insured — were set to expire. The cost to extend them? Twenty-five billion dollars.

Congress found twenty-seven billion for other countries' defense. They couldn't find twenty-five billion to keep Americans insured.

The subsidies expired December 31st, 2025. The Senate said there was — and I'm quoting — "no appetite" to extend them. Twenty-two million Americans are watching their premiums double right now. Today. Some are losing coverage entirely.

Twenty-seven million Americans have no health insurance at all.

Let me put those numbers next to each other.

Foreign military aid: twenty-seven billion. ACA extension cost: twenty-five billion. Uninsured Americans: twenty-seven million. Americans losing coverage: twenty-two million.

The money existed. The will didn't.

Now bring simufilam back into the picture.

A potential Alzheimer's drug was killed by short sellers. The FDA called their petition improper. The trial data was buried. The company was dismantled. Six million Americans have Alzheimer's. Someone new is diagnosed every sixty-five seconds.

Where was the system? Where was the twenty-seven billion dollars when an oral pill that could treat root-cause Alzheimer's was being strangled in its crib?

The system didn't fail. The system worked exactly as designed. It prioritized foreign military contracts over American healthcare. It allowed financial predators to destroy drug development for profit. And when the damage was done, it shrugged.

So here's the question nobody in Washington wants to answer:

If we can find twenty-seven billion dollars for other countries' wars, why can't we find a fraction of that to keep Americans alive?

I have a proposal. It's simple. It's one sentence.

**For every dollar the United States spends on foreign military aid, a minimum of two dollars must be invested in American healthcare.**

That's it. The foreign aid number sets a floor. Not a formula — a floor. Domestic healthcare spending can always go higher. It only pushes in one direction: up, for Americans.

At twenty-seven billion in foreign military aid, the floor would be fifty-four billion for American healthcare.

That covers the ACA subsidies. It covers the Medicaid gap. It funds NIH research — the kind of research that develops drugs like simufilam. And it still leaves headroom.

Why two dollars? Because one-to-one is parity — the bare minimum. Five-to-one sounds extreme and gives opponents an easy attack. Two-to-one is common sense. Twice as much on your own people as on someone else's military. That's not isolationism. That's priorities.

This isn't left or right. Every American has a grandmother. Every American knows someone with Alzheimer's, with cancer, with a condition they can't afford to treat. Every American is one diagnosis away from finding out what the system actually thinks of them.

The people who killed simufilam profited. The people who expired the subsidies got re-elected. The people losing their insurance right now — they got nothing.

The American Priority Act. Two-to-one. Healthcare floor. One sentence.

Share this video. Tag your representative. Tag your senator. Ask them the twenty-seven billion dollar question:

Why do other countries' armies come before American lives?
