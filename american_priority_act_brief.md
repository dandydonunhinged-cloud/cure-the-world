# THE AMERICAN PRIORITY ACT
## One-Page Policy Brief

---

### THE PROPOSAL

For every $1 the United States spends on foreign military aid, a minimum of $2 must be allocated to domestic healthcare investment.

Foreign military aid spending establishes a **floor** — not a ceiling, not a formula — for domestic healthcare. Domestic spending can always exceed the floor. The mechanism only pushes in one direction: toward Americans.

---

### THE MATH

| Category | Amount | Source |
|----------|--------|--------|
| US foreign military aid (FY2024) | $27.0B | Congressional Research Service |
| 2:1 floor → domestic healthcare minimum | **$54.0B** | Calculated |
| ACA enhanced subsidy extension cost | $25.0B | Congressional Budget Office |
| Current uninsured Americans | 27.0M | Census Bureau CPS 2024 |
| Americans losing coverage (subsidy expiration) | 22.0M | KFF Marketplace Analysis |

The $54B floor covers: ACA subsidy extension ($25B) + Medicaid coverage gap ($8-12B) + NIH research funding increase ($5-8B) + remaining headroom for state innovation grants and drug development incentives.

---

### WHY 2:1

- **1:1 (parity):** Bare minimum. Weak statement. Easily dismissed as symbolic.
- **2:1 (proposed):** Common-sense ratio. Twice as much on your own citizens as on foreign military. Defensible across political spectrum.
- **5:1 or higher:** Sounds extreme. Gives opponents easy attack surface. Frames proponents as isolationist.

The 2:1 ratio is the Goldilocks number — high enough to be meaningful, low enough to be inarguable.

---

### MECHANISM

1. Annual foreign military aid appropriations are calculated per existing process
2. The domestic healthcare floor is automatically set at 2x that figure
3. If healthcare spending already exceeds the floor, no action required
4. If healthcare spending falls below the floor, mandatory supplemental appropriation is triggered
5. Floor funds are allocated across: (a) marketplace subsidies, (b) Medicaid expansion, (c) NIH/BARDA research, (d) state healthcare innovation grants

---

### PRECEDENT

- **Davis-Bacon Act (1931):** Establishes wage floors on federal projects. Floor mechanism, not ceiling.
- **Maintenance of Effort (MOE) requirements:** Federal grants routinely require states to maintain minimum spending levels. Same concept, federal self-application.
- **PAYGO rules:** Congress already uses automatic mechanisms to enforce fiscal discipline. This applies the same logic to healthcare prioritization.

---

### ANTICIPATED OPPOSITION & RESPONSES

| Attack | Response |
|--------|----------|
| "This is isolationism" | No. Foreign aid continues unchanged. This only ensures Americans aren't deprioritized. The floor doesn't reduce foreign spending — it elevates domestic spending. |
| "It constrains Congressional flexibility" | Congress retains full control over foreign aid levels. The floor follows their own decisions. They set the number; the floor follows. |
| "Healthcare spending is already higher than $54B" | Then the floor is non-binding and costs nothing. It only activates when Congress lets healthcare fall below 2x military aid — which is the exact scenario it's designed to prevent. |
| "This creates an automatic spending increase" | Only if Congress chooses to increase foreign military aid. The mechanism is self-limiting: higher foreign aid = higher domestic floor. Congress controls both levers. |
| "Foreign aid serves national security" | Agreed. So does a healthy population. A nation that can't keep its own citizens insured has a domestic security crisis. The 2:1 ratio acknowledges both priorities while ensuring Americans come first. |
| "The math is too simple" | That's the point. A policy Americans can explain at a dinner table is a policy Americans will fight for. Complexity is where accountability goes to die. |

---

### THE CONTEXT (Why Now)

- ACA enhanced subsidies expired December 31, 2025
- Senate leadership stated "no appetite" for extension
- 22 million Americans facing premium increases of 50-100%
- Simultaneously, $27B in foreign military aid continued without debate
- NIH-funded drug development (e.g., simufilam/Alzheimer's) remains vulnerable to market manipulation with no protective mechanism
- Bipartisan polling consistently shows 70%+ support for prioritizing domestic healthcare over foreign aid
