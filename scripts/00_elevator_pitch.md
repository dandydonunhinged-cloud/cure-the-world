## SCRIPT

What if I told you there was a drug — an oral pill, not a $26,000-a-year IV infusion — that showed signs of slowing Alzheimer's disease in confirmed patients?

And what if I told you it was destroyed — not by science, not by failure — but by a coordinated short-selling campaign designed to profit from its death?

In August 2021, a group of short sellers filed a citizen petition with the FDA to halt the clinical trials of simufilam, a drug developed by Cassava Sciences. The stock dropped 55% in days. They made millions.

The FDA reviewed the petition. And denied it. Said it was "not the appropriate subject of a citizen petition."

But the damage was done.

The Phase 3 trials ran — contaminated. Twenty-one percent of the patients in one trial didn't even have Alzheimer's disease. A flawed blood test let them through. That's like testing a cancer drug where one in five patients don't have cancer — and then calling the drug a failure.

Even with that contamination, even with the trials terminated early — 22% of patients still active — there was still a signal. Patients with confirmed Alzheimer's showed improvement.

But the data that could prove it? Collected but never released.

The academic they accused of fraud? All charges dropped with prejudice. DOJ dismissed after jury selection. Cannot refile. Their justification evaporated.

But it didn't matter. The company's CEO was forced out. The short sellers are now suing the company for fighting back. Cassava announced it's ending the Alzheimer's program by June 30, 2026. Stock at $2.13 — was over $100. And big pharma is waiting for the patents to expire so they can pick up the research — for free.

Six million Americans have Alzheimer's. Every 65 seconds, someone new is diagnosed. And a potential treatment was killed for profit.

The receipts are all here. Every claim sourced. Every fact verified.

The question is: what are we going to do about it?
