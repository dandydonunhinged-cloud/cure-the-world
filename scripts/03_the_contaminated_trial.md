# EXPLAINER 3 — "The Contaminated Trial"
# Target: 3-4 minutes
# Topic: Trial design, 21% contamination, what it means for results

---

## SCRIPT

Let's talk about what "failed" actually means.

Cassava Sciences ran two Phase 3 trials. RETHINK tested 100 milligrams versus placebo in 804 patients over 52 weeks. REFOCUS tested two doses — 50 milligrams and 100 milligrams versus placebo — in 1,125 patients over 76 weeks.

To get into these trials, you needed a diagnosis of mild-to-moderate Alzheimer's disease. And to confirm that diagnosis biologically, the trials used a blood test — plasma p-tau181.

This was a first. No Phase 3 Alzheimer's trial had ever relied primarily on a plasma biomarker to confirm disease. The gold standard is an amyloid PET scan — a brain imaging test that directly shows amyloid plaques. But PET scans are expensive and not available everywhere. The blood test was supposed to be good enough.

It wasn't.

REFOCUS included an amyloid PET sub-study — 160 patients who got both the blood test AND the brain scan. The results:

33 out of 160 patients — 21% — were amyloid-negative at baseline. They did not have Alzheimer's disease pathology in their brains. The blood test let them through.

The peer-reviewed paper states it directly: "The plasma p-tau181 entry criterion did not reliably exclude amyloid PET negativity in the sub-study."

Now do the math. If 21% of 160 patients in the sub-study didn't have Alzheimer's, extrapolate across the full trial. REFOCUS had 1,125 patients. RETHINK had 804. If the same contamination rate held across both trials, roughly 400 patients across the two studies may not have had the disease the drug was designed to treat.

You cannot show a drug works for Alzheimer's if a fifth of your patients don't have Alzheimer's.

This is not a drug failure. This is a trial design problem. And here's what's critical: despite this contamination, there was still signal.

The paper reports that in a post-hoc analysis, patients with the highest p-tau181 levels — the top half, meaning the patients most likely to actually have Alzheimer's — showed nominally significant improvement in RETHINK at weeks 4, 28, and 40.

The sicker the patient was confirmed to be, the more the drug appeared to help.

Strip out the patients who shouldn't have been there. Let the trial run its full course with properly screened participants. You could be looking at a very different headline.

But that trial will never happen now.
