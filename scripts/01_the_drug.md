## SCRIPT

Before we talk about what was done to this drug, you need to understand what this drug is — and why it terrified certain people.

Simufilam is a small molecule that targets a protein called filamin A.

In a healthy brain, filamin A acts as scaffolding — it holds cellular structures in place. But in Alzheimer's disease, filamin A becomes altered. Its shape changes. And when that happens, it triggers a cascade: tau proteins become hyperphosphorylated. Neuroinflammation increases. Amyloid plaques accumulate.

Most Alzheimer's drugs — Leqembi, Aduhelm — go after the amyloid plaques directly. They're monoclonal antibodies. They require IV infusion. They cost over $26,000 a year. And they come with a risk of brain swelling so serious it has its own acronym — ARIA.

Simufilam takes a different approach. It binds to altered filamin A and restores its normal shape. Instead of mopping up the mess downstream, it fixes the thing causing the mess.

And it's an oral pill. Twice a day. Cheap to manufacture. No IV clinics. No ARIA risk.

A 2023 peer-reviewed study published with 16 citations confirmed that simufilam reverses filamin A alteration in human neuronal cells. This isn't theoretical. The mechanism is published and replicated.

Now think about what that means for the Alzheimer's treatment market.

Leqembi brought in $855 million in its first full year. The global Alzheimer's drug market is projected to reach $13 billion by 2028.

An oral pill that addresses root cause instead of symptoms? That could be manufactured generically? That doesn't require infusion centers?

That's not a competitor. That's an extinction event for the current business model.

Keep that in mind as we go through what happened next.
