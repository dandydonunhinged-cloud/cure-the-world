# RPCCP STRESS TEST: The American Priority Act
## Opposition Attack Vectors — Pre-Answered

Date: 2026-03-12
Method: Recursive opposition modeling. Every attack the other side will use.

---

## PASS 1: SURFACE-LEVEL POLITICAL ATTACKS

### Attack: "This is isolationist / America First rebranded"
**Strength: 6/10.** Easy to deploy, hard to make stick.
**Pre-answer:** Foreign aid doesn't change by a single dollar. The mechanism only adds domestic spending. Calling it isolationist requires arguing that insuring Americans is somehow anti-global. Good luck with that on camera.

### Attack: "You're using sick people as political props"
**Strength: 4/10.** Standard deflection.
**Pre-answer:** Twenty-two million people losing insurance aren't props. They're the subject. If acknowledging their existence is "using" them, what do you call ignoring them?

### Attack: "This is a backdoor spending increase"
**Strength: 7/10.** This is the real one. Fiscal hawks will hammer this.
**Pre-answer:** The floor only activates when healthcare falls below 2x foreign military aid. If Congress already spends above the floor, the mechanism is dormant. It's a safety net, not a spending mandate. And Congress controls the trigger — they set the foreign aid number. Want a lower floor? Spend less on foreign military aid.

---

## PASS 2: STRUCTURAL / LEGAL ATTACKS

### Attack: "This is unconstitutional — you can't bind future Congresses"
**Strength: 8/10.** The strongest structural objection.
**Pre-answer:** PAYGO rules, sequestration, debt ceiling mechanisms — Congress already binds itself with automatic fiscal triggers. The American Priority Act uses the same mechanism. It's a self-imposed rule, not a constitutional amendment. Any future Congress can repeal it — just like they can repeal PAYGO. But they'd have to do it on the record, publicly choosing foreign military aid over American healthcare. That's the accountability mechanism.

### Attack: "Foreign aid and healthcare are different appropriations — you can't link them"
**Strength: 5/10.** Procedural, not substantive.
**Pre-answer:** Congress links appropriations constantly. Defense spending triggers VA spending requirements. Highway bills link gas tax revenue to infrastructure. The mechanism is standard. The application is new.

### Attack: "The 2:1 ratio is arbitrary"
**Strength: 3/10.** Weak because every ratio is "arbitrary" until it becomes law.
**Pre-answer:** Minimum wage is arbitrary. Tax brackets are arbitrary. The 2:1 ratio is a starting point for negotiation. If opponents think 3:1 is better, let's hear it. The debate about the ratio is a win — it means they've accepted the principle.

---

## PASS 3: ECONOMIC / WONK ATTACKS

### Attack: "Healthcare spending is already $4.5 trillion — $54B is a rounding error"
**Strength: 6/10.** Technically true but misses the point.
**Pre-answer:** The $54B floor isn't about total spending — it's about the specific programs that fall through the cracks. ACA subsidies were $25B. They expired. Congress said "no appetite." The floor prevents that specific failure mode. Total healthcare spending includes private insurance, out-of-pocket, everything. The floor targets the government's share of keeping its citizens insured.

### Attack: "This will cause foreign aid to be cut to lower the floor"
**Strength: 7/10.** Perverse incentive argument. Smart opponents will use this.
**Pre-answer:** Possible. And if Congress decides to cut foreign military aid to lower the domestic healthcare floor, that's a policy choice voters can evaluate. The mechanism makes the tradeoff visible. Right now, the tradeoff is invisible — that's the problem. Transparency is a feature, not a bug.

### Attack: "NIH funding and drug development are separate issues from insurance coverage"
**Strength: 4/10.** True but irrelevant to the framing.
**Pre-answer:** The SAVA case shows they're connected. NIH-funded research produced simufilam's foundational science. Market manipulation killed the clinical program. Insufficient regulatory protection allowed it. The American Priority Act's floor funds include NIH/BARDA research — because keeping Americans alive requires both insurance AND new treatments.

---

## PASS 4: MEDIA / NARRATIVE ATTACKS

### Attack: "This is Reddit conspiracy theory / meme stock politics"
**Strength: 5/10.** Dismissive framing that works on people who haven't read the DD.
**Pre-answer:** Every claim in the DD is sourced to SEC filings, FDA documents, ClinicalTrials.gov, court records, and peer-reviewed publications. The elevator pitch explicitly says "verify everything." If the counter-argument is "don't look at the evidence," that proves the thesis.

### Attack: "You're conflating SAVA stock speculation with healthcare policy"
**Strength: 6/10.** The join point is the weakest link.
**Pre-answer:** The SAVA case is an illustration, not the foundation. The policy stands without it. But the illustration matters: a system that can't protect drug development AND can't keep citizens insured AND finds $27B for foreign military has a priorities problem. SAVA makes it tangible. The math makes it undeniable.

### Attack: "The $27B vs $25B comparison is misleading — foreign aid serves different purposes"
**Strength: 5/10.** Apples-to-oranges defense.
**Pre-answer:** Correct — they serve different purposes. One keeps foreign militaries armed. The other keeps Americans insured. The question isn't whether they serve the same purpose. The question is which one Congress chose when they could only do one. They chose the foreign military. The American Priority Act says: you don't get to make that choice anymore. You fund both, or you fund Americans first.

---

## PASS 5: THE KILL SHOT THEY'LL ACTUALLY USE

### Attack: "This will never pass. It's performative."
**Strength: 9/10.** This is what they'll say to make people stop pushing.
**Pre-answer:** The ACA was "never going to pass" until it did. Gay marriage was "never going to happen" until it did. The minimum wage increase was "dead on arrival" for a decade until states started doing it themselves. Every structural reform starts as "performative." The question is whether enough people care enough to make it non-performative. Twenty-two million people just lost their insurance. Six million have Alzheimer's. The audience exists. The energy exists. The only thing missing is the ask. This is the ask.

## VULNERABILITY ASSESSMENT

| Vector | Threat Level | Mitigation |
|--------|:---:|-----------|
| Constitutional binding objection | HIGH | Frame as self-imposed rule (like PAYGO), not constitutional amendment |
| Fiscal hawk "spending increase" | HIGH | Emphasize floor is dormant when spending exceeds it; Congress controls trigger |
| Perverse incentive (cut aid to lower floor) | MEDIUM | Transparency argument — making the tradeoff visible is the point |
| "Performative" dismissal | HIGH | Historical precedent + 22M losing coverage = urgency counterweight |
| SAVA connection feels forced | MEDIUM | Policy stands alone; SAVA is the illustration that makes it personal |
| Ratio is arbitrary | LOW | All ratios are; 2:1 is a negotiation starting point |

## BOTTOM LINE

The strongest attacks are procedural (constitutional binding) and defeatist ("it'll never pass"). The weakest attacks are substantive — the math is clean, the comparison is valid, and the 2:1 ratio is defensible. The policy brief should lead with the math, not the SAVA story. SAVA is the hook for the video series. The policy brief stands on its own numbers.

The kill-shot counter to "it'll never pass": **"Then vote against it on the record. Tell your constituents you chose foreign military aid over their healthcare. We'll wait."**
