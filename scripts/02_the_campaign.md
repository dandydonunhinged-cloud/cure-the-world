# EXPLAINER 2 — "The Short-and-Distort Playbook"
# Target: 4-5 minutes
# Topic: The coordinated campaign — citizen petition, short selling, timeline

---

## SCRIPT

On August 18, 2021, an attorney filed a citizen petition with the FDA. The petition asked the agency to halt all clinical trials of simufilam.

Citizen petitions are a legitimate tool. They're meant to allow the public to raise safety concerns about drugs. But this one was different.

The attorney was working on behalf of clients who held short positions in Cassava Sciences stock. They stood to profit if the stock went down. And they had just filed the document designed to make that happen.

The petition contained a 42-page technical report authored by Dr. David Bredt, a neuroscientist, and Dr. Geoffrey Pitt, a cardiologist at Weill Cornell. The report alleged "a series of anomalies" in Cassava's published research and claimed it "strongly suggests systemic data manipulation."

Within days, the story was everywhere. The stock dropped 55%. Short sellers made millions.

Eight days later, on August 26, 2021, the law firm Labaton Sucharow publicly disclosed that it represented clients with short positions in Cassava Sciences — the same firm connected to the citizen petition.

Let that sink in. The petition was filed by people who had already placed their bets against the company. They weren't protecting patients. They were protecting their trades.

Now here's what happened next — and this is the part that matters.

The FDA reviewed the citizen petition. In February 2022, the FDA formally denied it. Their words: "not the appropriate subject of a citizen petition."

The FDA — the federal agency responsible for drug safety — looked at the allegations and said no.

But by then, the damage was done. The stock was gutted. The company was under SEC investigation. The narrative was set: Cassava Sciences was a fraud.

Cassava fought back. In November 2022, they filed a defamation lawsuit alleging the short sellers had made over 1,000 false and defamatory statements. They named Quintessential Capital Management, Dr. Bredt, Dr. Pitt, and others.

The court dismissed the lawsuit in March 2024. Reason: most of the statements were "protected under the First Amendment as statements of opinion or scientific debate."

So here's where we are: you can file a citizen petition on behalf of short-selling clients, the FDA can deny it as improper, the stock can crash 55%, and the courts will call your statements "protected opinion."

But it gets worse.

In June 2024, Dr. Hoau-Yan Wang — an academic collaborator who had worked on Cassava's preclinical research — was indicted for fraud related to NIH grants. This had nothing to do with the clinical trials. The Phase 3 trials were conducted at over 75 independent clinical sites across the US, Canada, Puerto Rico, and South Korea. But Wang's indictment poisoned everything.

In July 2024, CEO Remi Barbier and SVP Lindsay Burns were forced to resign. Barbier was barred from serving as an officer or director for three years and fined $175,000.

And in August 2024, Cassava dropped its own defamation lawsuit.

Then — and I want you to really hear this — the short sellers turned around and filed a malicious prosecution lawsuit against Cassava. Against Barbier. Against Burns. For having the nerve to sue them in the first place.

In March 2025, a federal judge ruled the lawsuit could proceed.

And here's the final twist. In October 2025, the DOJ dropped all charges against Wang. With prejudice. After jury selection. Meaning they can never refile. CUNY's own investigation found image issues in his work but said they fell short of scientific misconduct.

The one piece of evidence the short sellers pointed to — the thing that retroactively justified their campaign — evaporated. It didn't matter. By then, the CEO was gone, the lawsuits were filed, the stock was at $2.13, and the company had announced it would end the Alzheimer's program entirely by June 30, 2026.

The people who filed a petition the FDA rejected. Who shorted the stock before filing it. Who made millions from the crash. Whose key justification was dismissed with prejudice. They are now suing the people who tried to cure Alzheimer's disease.
