# EXPLAINER 5 — "The Patent Play — Who Profits When the Cure Dies?"
# Target: 3-4 minutes
# Topic: Patent vulnerability, big pharma endgame, Yale license

---

## SCRIPT

Here's the part where you follow the money all the way to the end.

On January 7, 2025 — weeks after the trials were terminated — the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office issued patent number 12,186,307 to Yale University. It covers simufilam as a method of treatment. Yale exclusively licensed it to Cassava Sciences.

The license terms: a nominal upfront fee, potential milestones up to $4.5 million, and low-to-mid single digit royalties on net sales.

Cassava pivoted. They're now focused on using simufilam for seizures related to tuberous sclerosis complex — a rare neurodevelopmental disorder. A 2020 paper in Science Translational Medicine showed simufilam meaningfully reduced seizure frequency in an animal model.

But here's the problem. Cassava is a company on life support.

Stock price: $2.13, down from over $100. The CEO is barred from serving for three years. They're paying out $31.25 million in a shareholder class action settlement. They're defendants in a malicious prosecution case. They've announced they'll end the Alzheimer's program by June 30, 2026. Their cash is bleeding.

Oh — and the academic fraud case that gave the short sellers their cover? All charges dropped with prejudice in October 2025. DOJ dismissed after jury selection. Cannot refile. The one justification they had? Gone.

University licenses have diligence clauses. If the licensee can't fund development — can't hit milestones, can't maintain the program — the university can reclaim the license and offer it to someone else.

Yale is a university. They don't develop drugs. They want licensing revenue. If Cassava can't perform, Yale will find someone who can. And Pfizer, Moderna, Lilly — they have the checkbook.

But here's the thing — they might not even need the patent.

The filamin A mechanism is now published in peer-reviewed literature. The Phase 3 clinical data is public. The subgroup analysis showing efficacy in confirmed patients is public. All of the hard, expensive, risky work — identifying the target, proving the mechanism, running the trials — has been done. And paid for by Cassava and its shareholders.

A large pharmaceutical company can take that published science, develop a structurally different filamin A modulator, and bring it to market without ever touching Cassava's patent. Different molecule, same validated target. All the discovery risk eliminated. All the clinical insight available for free in the published literature.

This is the endgame. Tank the small company. Let it bleed out. Let the patents weaken or expire. Then harvest the science.

The shareholders funded the R&D. The patients took the risk. And if this plays out the way it's set up to play out, a company that had nothing to do with the discovery will sell the treatment at premium prices.

Unless somebody stops it.
