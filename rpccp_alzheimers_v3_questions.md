## THE QUESTIONS

### Q1: THE BBB PARADOX
**A drug that doesn't enter the brain changed biomarkers inside the brain. How?**

Semaglutide has negligible brain penetrance. Yet it reduced CSF pTau217 by 9%. The tau is INSIDE neurons, INSIDE the brain, behind the BBB. How does a drug that can't get in change what's happening inside?

Four candidate mechanisms:
- (a) **Circumventricular access** — GLP-1 enters through areas lacking BBB (area postrema, median eminence). These are small windows, not highways. Enough to drive a 10% biomarker change?
- (b) **Vagal signaling** — GLP-1R on vagal afferents in gut → nucleus of solitary tract → brain stem → widespread CNS effects. The brain never sees the drug molecule — just the nerve signal.
- (c) **Cerebrovascular** — GLP-1R on brain endothelial cells dilates arterioles, increases blood flow, improves glymphatic clearance. Better drainage = lower tau levels in CSF. Not neuronal rescue — plumbing improvement.
- (d) **Peripheral anti-inflammation** — Systemic GLP-1 effects reduce peripheral inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-6) that cross the BBB and drive neuroinflammation. Cut the fire from outside, the inside cools.

**Why this matters:** If the mechanism is (c) or (d), then brain penetrance is IRRELEVANT for GLP-1 neuroprotection. The drug doesn't need to enter the brain to protect it. This changes every assumption about CNS drug development for AD.

**Who should answer this:** The EVOKE investigators. They have 199 CSF samples. Correlate biomarker changes with peripheral inflammatory markers, cerebral blood flow measures, and cardiovascular outcomes. The data exists.

### Q2: THE ROUTE QUESTION
**Why did injected liraglutide preserve brain volume but oral semaglutide didn't improve cognition?**

| | ELAD (liraglutide) | EVOKE (semaglutide) |
|--|---|---|
| Route | Subcutaneous injection | Oral |
| N | 204 | 3,800 |
| Duration | 52 weeks | 156 weeks (3 years) |
| Brain volume | 50% less loss | Not reported prominently |
| Cognition | 18% slower decline | No difference from placebo |
| CSF biomarkers | Not reported | pTau reduced ~10% |
| Primary endpoint | MISSED (FDG-PET) | MISSED (CDR-SB) |

Possible explanations:
- **Molecule:** Liraglutide and semaglutide have different receptor binding kinetics and half-lives
- **Route:** Subcutaneous injection achieves higher peak plasma levels than oral; circumventricular organ exposure may scale with peak concentration, not AUC
- **Trial size:** ELAD's positive findings are from 204 patients. At 3,800, EVOKE is better powered to detect TRUE effects — and found none
- **Disease stage:** ELAD was mild-to-moderate; EVOKE was early-stage symptomatic. Different populations

**The uncomfortable question:** Is ELAD's brain volume finding a small-sample artifact that wouldn't replicate at scale? EVOKE's null result is the stronger evidence.

**Who should answer this:** Novo Nordisk (EVOKE) and Imperial College (ELAD) should run a HEAD-TO-HEAD comparison trial — same endpoints, same population, injected liraglutide vs oral semaglutide vs placebo. This would also test whether the route matters.

### Q3: THE APOE4 GLP-1 DEFICIENCY QUESTION
**If APOE4 carriers have reduced endogenous GLP-1, is GLP-1 agonist therapy replacement rather than treatment?**

This reframes everything. APOE4 is the strongest genetic risk factor for AD. APOE4 carriers have:
- Reduced GLP-1 levels
- Reduced insulin signaling (receptor trapped in endosomes)
- Reduced leptin, ghrelin, glucagon
- Impaired cerebral glucose metabolism decades before symptoms

If GLP-1 deficiency is part of the APOE4 risk profile, then GLP-1 agonists aren't "treating Alzheimer's" — they're **correcting a metabolic deficiency that contributes to Alzheimer's risk in genetically susceptible individuals.**

**Testable prediction:** Stratify EVOKE data by APOE4 status. APOE4 carriers should show LARGER biomarker improvements on semaglutide than non-carriers. If true, GLP-1 therapy should be targeted to APOE4+ individuals, not all AD patients.

**The deeper question:** Should APOE4+ individuals be put on GLP-1 agonists PREVENTIVELY, decades before symptoms, the way statin therapy is used for cardiovascular risk? What does the epidemiological data show for APOE4+ diabetics on GLP-1 agonists vs those on other antidiabetics?

**Who should answer this:** The Cleveland Clinic group that published the real-world GLP-1/AD risk reduction data. They have the database to stratify by APOE4 status.

### Q4: THE FILANA PARADOX
**If simufilam works for TSC seizures but failed for AD — what does that tell us about FLNA as a drug target?**

Cassava/Filana pivoted to TSC-related epilepsy. Preclinical results: 60% seizure reduction in mouse model. The mechanism: FLNA expression is increased in TSC → hyperactive mTOR → epilepsy. Simufilam normalizes FLNA → normalizes mTOR → fewer seizures.

Same drug. Same target (FLNA). Same downstream pathway (mTOR). Works in TSC. Fails in AD.

Possible explanations:
- **Single-gene vs multi-factorial:** TSC is caused by TSC1/TSC2 mutations. One cause, one target, clean response. AD has dozens of contributing pathways — fixing one (FLNA-mTOR) isn't enough.
- **Brain penetrance isn't the issue after all:** If simufilam reaches the brain enough to reduce seizures, it reaches the brain enough to modulate FLNA in AD. The Phase 3 failure isn't a delivery problem — it's a mechanism insufficiency problem.
- **Timing:** TSC patients are younger with less accumulated damage. AD patients have decades of pathology. The drug might prevent but not reverse.
- **FLNA's role differs between diseases:** In TSC, increased FLNA expression drives the disease. In AD, altered FLNA conformation drives the disease. Same protein, different pathology. The drug might fix one alteration but not the other.

**Why this matters:** If Filana's TSC proof-of-concept succeeds in humans (H1 2026), it proves simufilam reaches the brain and modulates FLNA. At that point, the AD failure is definitively a mechanism problem (FLNA alone isn't enough), not a delivery problem. That's actually useful negative evidence.

**Who should answer this:** Filana Therapeutics. If their TSC trial succeeds, they should run CSF FLNA and mTOR biomarker panels in AD patients on simufilam to confirm it's reaching the target in AD brain too.

### Q5: THE 10% CEILING
**Is 10% CSF biomarker reduction from peripheral mechanisms the ceiling? Or the floor?**

Semaglutide achieved ~10% reduction in pTau and neuroinflammation markers from outside the BBB. Lecanemab (which enters the brain and directly clears amyloid) achieves ~25-30% reduction in comparable markers.

Is 10% the maximum a peripheral drug can achieve? Or would higher doses, longer treatment, or different GLP-1 molecules push it further?

**What this tells us about peripheral vs central approaches:**
- If 10% is the ceiling → peripheral mechanisms can slow but not stop AD
- If 10% is dose-dependent and can increase → peripheral approaches might reach clinically meaningful thresholds
- If the 10% is MOSTLY from one mechanism (e.g., cerebral blood flow) → stacking multiple peripheral mechanisms could be additive

**Who should answer this:** Dose-response studies of GLP-1 agonists with CSF biomarker endpoints. Does doubling the dose double the biomarker effect?

### Q6: THE PREVENTION QUESTION
**Is GLP-1 a preventive that was tested as a treatment?**

[ALZFORUM headline](https://www.alzforum.org/news/conference-coverage/semaglutide-does-not-treat-alzheimers-could-it-prevent-dementia): "Semaglutide does not treat Alzheimer's. Could it prevent dementia?"

Epidemiological data: GLP-1 agonist users have REDUCED AD risk.
Clinical trial data: GLP-1 agonists DON'T improve cognition in existing AD.

This is the same pattern as statins and heart disease. Statins prevent heart attacks brilliantly. They don't reverse existing heart damage.

**The implication:** If GLP-1 prevents but doesn't treat, then the METABOLIC-TO-NEURODEGENERATION cascade is a ONE-WAY valve. Once metabolic dysfunction has transduced into structural brain damage (via whatever mechanism — FLNA, direct amyloid toxicity, vascular damage), fixing the metabolism can't undo the structural damage.

**The trial that should be running:** GLP-1 agonist in PRESYMPTOMATIC APOE4+ individuals with elevated amyloid PET but normal cognition. 5-year endpoint: conversion to clinical AD. This is a prevention trial, not a treatment trial.

**Who should answer this:** Novo Nordisk has the drug and the money. The Alzheimer's Prevention Initiative (API) has the infrastructure. The APOE4+ population is identifiable by genotyping. This trial should exist.

### Q7: THE COMBINATION NOBODY IS TESTING
**Why isn't there a GLP-1 + lecanemab trial?**

This is the most obvious combination in AD research and nobody is running it:
- GLP-1: peripheral neuroprotection (anti-inflammation, cerebral blood flow, tau reduction)
- Lecanemab: central amyloid clearance (IV antibody that crosses BBB and removes plaques)

Together: clear existing damage AND provide ongoing neuroprotection from the periphery.

The ADDF literally said EVOKE "suggests potential for combination therapies." Yet no GLP-1 + anti-amyloid combination trial is registered on ClinicalTrials.gov.

**Why not?**
- Different companies (Novo Nordisk vs Biogen/Eisai) — no incentive to combine
- Regulatory complexity of combination trials
- Neither drug alone showed enough efficacy to justify the cost
- Nobody has proposed it formally

**Who should answer this:** Academic medical centers running investigator-initiated trials. The drugs are both available. ADCS (Alzheimer's Disease Cooperative Study) or ACTC (Alzheimer's Clinical Trials Consortium) could design this.

### Q8: THE CEREBROVASCULAR QUESTION
**Is Alzheimer's a vascular disease with neurodegeneration as a symptom?**

GLP-1R dilates cerebral arterioles. Liraglutide preserved brain volume. The glymphatic system (brain's waste clearance) depends on cerebrovascular function. Vascular risk factors (hypertension, diabetes, atherosclerosis) are the strongest modifiable risk factors for AD.

What if the entire amyloid/tau/inflammation framework is DOWNSTREAM of cerebrovascular dysfunction?

```
Vascular risk factors (diabetes, hypertension, APOE4)
        ↓
Cerebrovascular dysfunction (reduced blood flow, BBB breakdown)
        ↓
Impaired glymphatic clearance + reduced nutrient delivery
        ↓
Amyloid accumulation (can't clear it) + metabolic stress (can't feed neurons)
        ↓
Tau phosphorylation + neuroinflammation
        ↓
Neurodegeneration
```

If this is right:
- Anti-amyloid drugs are mopping the floor with the faucet running
- GLP-1 drugs improve the plumbing (why liraglutide preserved volume)
- Exercise works for the same reason (cerebral blood flow)
- Blood pressure control works for the same reason

**The test:** Compare the AD risk reduction from GLP-1 agonists vs aggressive blood pressure control vs regular exercise vs anti-amyloid therapy. If vascular interventions consistently outperform anti-amyloid interventions in PREVENTION, the vascular hypothesis is dominant.

---

### Q9: THE GLYMPHATIC QUESTION
**Does GLP-1 improve brain waste clearance through the glymphatic system?**

The glymphatic system clears amyloid and tau from the brain during sleep. It depends on:
- Cerebrovascular pulsation (drives CSF flow through brain parenchyma)
- Aquaporin-4 water channels on astrocyte endfeet
- Adequate sleep (glymphatic clearance increases 60% during sleep)

GLP-1R activation increases cerebrovascular pulsation through arteriolar dilation. If GLP-1 drugs boost glymphatic clearance, that would explain:
- CSF tau reduction (better clearance from brain → lower steady-state levels in CSF... wait, actually higher in CSF if clearance improves. Need to think about this more carefully.)
- Brain volume preservation (less toxic accumulation → less neuronal death)
- Why it prevents but doesn't treat (can't clear damage already done to dead neurons)

**The test:** GLP-1 agonist + MRI glymphatic imaging (DTI-ALPS or intrathecal gadolinium tracer) pre- and post-treatment. Does GLP-1 increase glymphatic clearance rate?

**Who should answer this:** Maiken Nedergaard's lab (University of Rochester / University of Copenhagen) — the group that discovered the glymphatic system. This is directly in their wheelhouse.

### Q10: THE META-QUESTION
**Why do all these fields — GLP-1, amyloid, tau, FLNA, vascular, glymphatic, APOE — exist as separate research programs?**

The answer is structural: different labs, different funding streams, different conferences, different journals. Novo Nordisk doesn't attend CTAD (Clinical Trials on Alzheimer's Disease). Filana Therapeutics doesn't attend endocrinology meetings. The glymphatic researchers are in neurovascular biology. The APOE4 researchers are in genetics.

RPCCP's value isn't discovering new biology. It's forcing COLLISION between fields that don't collide naturally.

The questions in this document aren't radical. They're OBVIOUS — once you put the findings next to each other. The fact that they haven't been asked tells you something about how science is organized, not about how science works.

## THE ONE-PAGE VERSION

If I had to put this in front of one person, it would say:

**The EVOKE trial data already contains the answer to Question 3.** 3,800 patients. APOE4 status recorded at enrollment. CSF biomarkers measured in 199 patients. Semaglutide reduced tau by ~10% despite not crossing the BBB.

**Run one analysis:** Stratify the CSF biomarker substudy by APOE4 carrier status.

If APOE4 carriers show LARGER biomarker improvements → GLP-1 deficiency is part of the APOE4 risk profile, and GLP-1 agonist therapy should be tested as PREVENTION in presymptomatic APOE4+ individuals.

If APOE4 carriers show NO DIFFERENCE → the metabolic pathway is less important than proposed, and the field should focus on vascular and glymphatic mechanisms instead.

**One stratification. Existing data. No new drug. No new trial. Just the question nobody asked.**
