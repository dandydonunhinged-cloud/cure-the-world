## SCRIPT

So here's where we are.

A drug that targets the root cause of Alzheimer's — not the symptoms, the cause — showed signal in confirmed patients despite trials contaminated with people who didn't have the disease. The trials were killed early. The critical imaging data was collected but never released. The company was systematically dismantled by a campaign the FDA itself rejected. And the short sellers who did it are suing the people who tried to develop the cure.

Twelve point eight two percent of the float is still short. Six point one three million shares. Eleven days to cover.

The shorts are still betting this company dies.

Here's what they're not counting on: us.

This isn't about a short squeeze. This isn't about memes. This is about whether we allow a system where short sellers can kill drug development for diseases that affect six million Americans — and profit from doing it.

Here's what can actually be done. And every one of these is concrete.

**One: Demand the data.**

The week-76 amyloid PET scans were collected in REFOCUS. They show whether simufilam cleared amyloid from the brains of confirmed Alzheimer's patients. That data exists. Shareholders — current and former — have a legitimate interest in its release. An independent data safety monitoring board reviewed this trial. The data is there.

**Two: Support independent reanalysis.**

The Phase 3 results are published. An independent biostatistician can reanalyze the data excluding the 21% of patients confirmed amyloid-negative at baseline. If the signal strengthens — and the subgroup data suggests it will — that's a publishable paper that changes the entire narrative.

**Three: Congressional attention.**

The short-and-distort playbook is not unique to Cassava. Small biotechs developing treatments for Alzheimer's, ALS, rare cancers — they're all vulnerable. A single Congressional hearing on how citizen petitions are weaponized by short sellers to kill clinical trials would expose the playbook and potentially lead to regulatory reform. The Senate Banking Committee and the Senate Health Committee both have jurisdiction.

**Four: Patient advocacy.**

1,081 patients were in the open-label extension. 630 of them were cut off by sponsor decision. Some of them may have been experiencing benefit. Alzheimer's patient advocacy organizations — the Alzheimer's Association, UsAgainstAlzheimer's — should be asking: where is the data? What happened to those patients? Do their families know what was collected and never reported?

**Five: Hold the line.**

If you believe the science was real and the system failed, the stock is the mechanism. At $2.13 a share with 12.82% short interest and 11 days to cover, retail pressure has asymmetric upside. Not because of a squeeze — because if the company survives long enough to advance the TSC program, to find a partner, to release the buried data — the story changes.

This is not financial advice. I'm telling you what the facts are and what actions are available. You make your own decisions.

But I'll say this: GameStop was about a video game store. This is about your grandmother. Your mother. Your father. Six million people losing their memories right now, today, while a potential treatment sits buried under lawsuits and short positions.

We've done this before. We can do it again. And this time, it actually matters.
